Culture of Russia in the XIV-XVI centuries

At the end of the XIV century there began the revival of Russian culture. The victories over the Golden Horde contributed to the rise of national identity. The struggle against foreign conquerors led to the fact that it was the idea of patriotism that took the leading place in the culture of Russia.

After the Turks’ conquest of Constantinople in 1453 and the fall of the Byzantine Empire, Russia began to perceive itself as the center of global Orthodoxy, which influenced the country's cultural development.

The Tale of Mamai's Debacle © shakko, CC BY‑SA 3.0

In the XVI century, chronicle writing was developing. The chronicles were also focused on the theme of patriotism and unity of the Russian lands. “The Tale of the Destruction of Riazan” , “The Tale of Mamay’s Battle” and others were devoted to the struggle against foreign conquerors.

In the last quarter of the XV century, there appeared notes about Tver merchant Afanasy Nikitin’s journey to India (“Journey Beyond Three Seas” ).

The norms of a person’s behavior in the XVI century, his responsibilities in the family and society were described in the book, which was called "Domostroy" .

An important milestone in the development of culture was the appearance of printing in the era of Ivan IV. In 1563, a printing house was established in Moscow. It was headed by Ivan Fyodorov . In 1564, the first Russian book “The Apostle” was published.

In the late XV - early XVI century, there was completed the construction of the Moscow Kremlin . Masters from Russia and Italy took part in its creation.

In the territory of the Kremlin, there was built the Assumption Cathedral (architect Aristotele Fioravanti), which was built after the fashion of the Assumption Cathedral in Vladimir, but it was much larger.

Cathedrals of the Moscow Kremlin

Another architectural monument of the Moscow Kremlin is the Archangel Cathedral (1506-1508, architect Aleviz Novyi); it was the tomb of Moscow princes and tsars (until the XVIII century). The Kremlin became the symbol of the greatness and power of Moscow as the capital of the unified Russian state.

Modern look of the Moscow Kremlin © Aleksandr_Mullin, CC BY‑SA 3.0

In the middle of the XVI century, by order of Ivan IV, on Red Square there was built Pokrovsky Cathedral , the most famous cathedral in Moscow. Later, it was called St. Basil's Cathedral .

The heyday of medieval Russian painting is associated with the name of Andrei Rublev - the author of the world famous icon "The Trinity" (today it can be seen in the State Tretyakov Gallery, Moscow).
