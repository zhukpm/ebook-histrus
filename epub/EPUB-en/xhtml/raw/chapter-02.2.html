Socio-economic development of Russia in the XV - the first half of the XVI century

In that era Russia was an agricultural country with a significant predominance of the rural population (90% of the population were peasants). Harvests were very low due to the harsh climate and barren land in Russia. But the unification of peasants into communities, mutual assistance and Christian morality, which taught people to live not for wealth, but according to conscience, helped peasants to survive together. Peasants built their houses from wood. They heated houses with firewood with the help of a stove, which was also used for cooking.

In the first half of the XVI century, the size of peasant family grew. It gave the economy the necessary labor power and created conditions for the growth of production. At the same time, the social and legal status of farmers improved. This is evidenced by the fact of the spread of the term "peasants" (Christians). Peasants had the right to freely leave the master only once a year, on the day of the holiday, called St. George's Day.

At the beginning of the XVI century, there were developing cities in Russia (Moscow, Novgorod, Tver, Yaroslavl, Nizhny Novgorod, etc.). But their population, except for Moscow, which totaled about 100 thousand inhabitants, was small. The cities became centers of craft and commerce. In Moscow, there emerged state manufacture for the production of weapons. However, Russia lagged behind the West, and its economy needed the development of maritime trade with European countries.
