Political fragmentation of Ancient Rus (XII-XIII centuries)

By the end of the XII century, there had emerged about 15 small states-principalities(the Principality of Kiev, Vladimir-Suzdal principality, the Principality of Chernigov, the Principality of Smolensk and other principalities, as well as Novgorod Land). However, there remained close ties between individual principalities thanks to the common faith and language. In general, the political fragmentation weakened Rus.

North-Eastern Rus (Vladimir-Suzdal principality) got out of Kievâ€™s rule in the 30s of the XII century under Prince Yuri Dolgoruky (he got this name for interfering in the affairs of the neighboring principalities). He is considered the founder of Moscow (1147 - the first mention of Moscow in the annals). After the death of Yuri Dolgoruky, the power passed to his son Andrei Bogolyubsky (1157-1174). He moved the capital to Vladimir and wanted to turn it into the center of all Russian lands. This is evidenced by the construction of the Assumption Cathedral there, which was higher than the Cathedral of St. Sophia in Kiev.

In the North-West of Rus, there was situated Novgorod. It was the largest trade center. Novgorodians sold furs, honey, leather and other goods to European countries.

At the beginning of the XII century, Novgorodians achieved complete independence. Therefore, the city was called Free Novgorod. The supreme authority there was the veche (assembly). However, Novgorod continued to invite princes from other lands to serve. At the same time, the prince performed only military and police functions, but he did not have the right to interfere in the internal affairs of the city management.
