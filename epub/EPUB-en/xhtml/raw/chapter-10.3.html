Foreign policy of the Russian Federation

The Russian Federation pursued its foreign policy in difficult international conditions. Russia had lost its allies in Eastern and Central Europe. Near the borders of the CIS, armed conflicts took place. The government set a task to restore the position of Russia as a strong state. Russia remained the permanent member of the Security Council of the United Nations (UN). It took over the control over the Soviet nuclear weapons.

There were developing the relations of the Russian Federation with the countries of the West, primarily the USA.

Due to the collapse of the USSR and the communist system, the Cold War ended. Russia and the USA said they no longer considered each other as enemies. Russia supported the Americans in the fight against world terrorism, and along with Western countries it fought against drug trafficking.

In 1996 , Russia became a member of the Council of Europe.

The economic cooperation of the Russian Federation with foreign countries expanded. There were developing ties of Russia with the CIS countries. In 1993 , the CIS Charter was adopted. In 1995, there was established the Customs Union between Russia and Belarus; Kazakhstan also joined it. In 1999 , an agreement was signed on the formation of the Union State of Russia and Belarus . There were concluded agreements on military cooperation of Russia with Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan.

However, during that period integration within the CIS was developing in an extremely controversial way. Russia faced the task of strengthening its influence within the CIS and developing ties with the CIS countries.
