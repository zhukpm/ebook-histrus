Struggle of Rus against external invasions in the XIII century

At the beginning of the XIII century, in Central Asia, the Mongol tribes, that had united, created a powerful state under the leadership of Genghis Khan and began their conquests.

In 1219, Genghis Khan's troops captured Central Asia. By 1243, there had been captured the territory of Transcaucasia. In 1237 one of Genghis Khan’s descendants, Batu Khan, invaded Russia. In December 1240, the Mongols were able to capture Kiev, the capital of Ancient Rus. Eventually, despite the heroic defense, the enemy captured the Russian lands, because they had been weakened by the internal struggle. Thus, Rus bore the brunt of the nomads and provided favorable conditions for the development of Europe’s peoples.

At that time, the northwestern lands of Rus were threatened by a new danger - the Swedes and Germans. In 1240 , a Swedish detachment landed at the mouth of the Neva River.

Prince Alexander Yaroslavich, who at that time ruled in Novgorod, attacked the camp of the Swedes and defeated them. For his victory, he got the name "Nevsky". In 1240, Livonian (German) knights captured Pskov and attacked the Novgorod lands. In the spring of 1242 , Prince Alexander Nevsky defeated their detachment on the ice of Lake Peipsi/Chudskoe (“The Battle on the Ice”). This victory saved Rus from Western aggression and from the attempts to impose Catholicism instead of Orthodoxy on the Russian people.

In the XIII century, after the Mongol invasion, Rus became part of the Golden Horde, but it retained its internal practices and princely dynasties. The political dependence of Rus was that the Russian princes gained from the Khan of the Golden Horde the right to rule their lands (a label for Vladimir’s reign). Rus also paid him tribute (tax). The Orthodox Church was exempted from taxes.

The Mongol invasion claimed the lives of thousands of people. Many towns were destroyed. Cultural monuments also suffered. The invasion and dependence on the Golden Horde hindered the historical development of Rus.
