The first Russian revolution

The revolution began at the factories and plants of St. Petersburg in January 1905 . On the morning of January 9 (it was Sunday), ordinary people went to the tsar’s palace with a request to improve their living standards. The police blocked the way and began to shoot at unarmed people. This day was called "Bloody Sunday" because there were injured and killed many people.

In Russia, there began to intensify the revolutionary struggle of the people against the tsar and his government.

During the revolution, workers demanded an eight-hour workday, democratic freedoms; peasants demanded a solution to the problem of land. The military in the army were discontented that Russia had lost the Russo-Japanese war.

Especially a lot of people (two million people) participated in the strikes in October. There were people’s actions against the emperor and the government all over Russia. Therefore, this process was called the “All-Russian October Political Strike”. Throughout Russia, there began actions of workers and peasants.

Under the influence of people’s actions, on October 17, 1905 Emperor Nicholas II signed the Manifesto . It proclaimed freedom of speech, press and assembly; it allowed creating parties and unions. For the first time in the history of the country there began to operate a legislative body - the Russian parliament, which was called the State Duma . There were created all-Russian liberal political parties (Kadets, Octobrists) and monarchist organizations. Liberals opposed the revolution; they advocated democratic reforms. Monarchists defended the absolute power of the monarch and Russian national traditions.

By June 1907, the revolutionary movement had weakened. Thus, the First Russian revolution was over. The most important result of the revolution was the creation of the State Duma. Workers gained the right to create trade unions and conduct economic strikes. The agrarian reform began. However, the autocracy and landholding of landowners remained, albeit limited. Peasants continued to oppose landlords, and the working class and intelligentsia advocated extension of political freedoms.
