Russia’s foreign policy in the XVII century

Russia’s foreign policy in the XVII century

The main objective of Russia's foreign policy was the return of the territories that Russia had lost as a result of the Time of Troubles, as well as the annexation of Ukrainian and other lands that had been part of Ancient Rus. Moreover, Russia fought for the access to the Baltic Sea and the Black Sea, which was necessary to establish economic ties with Europe.

In 1648, in Ukraine, which was part of Poland, there began a national liberation uprising. The Ukrainian and Belarusian Orthodox populations suffered from exploitation by Polish lords. In addition, it experienced religious and national oppression by the Catholic Church.

The uprising against the Poles was led by Hetman Bohdan Khmelnitsky . In the autumn of 1653, Russia came to the aid of the rebels. The Zemsky Sobor of 1653 decided on Ukraine’s accession to the Russian state.

In January 1654 , in Pereyaslavlthere was convened the Ukrainian Council which advocated the accession of Ukraine to Russia. This association was based on the religious, ethnic and historical commonality of the Russian and Ukrainian peoples.

The decision of Ukraine’s accession to the Russian state led to the war of Russia against Poland (1654-1667). After its completion, Russia regained the lands that it had lost after the Time of Troubles (Smolensk and Novgorod-Seversk lands). It also gained the territory of Left-bank Ukraine and Kiev, which is located on the right bank of the Dnieper.

Russia’s eastern advance was much easier. During the XVII century, Russian explorers moved from Western Siberia to the shores of the Pacific Ocean. At the same time, there began peasant colonization of the arable lands in Southern Siberia.
