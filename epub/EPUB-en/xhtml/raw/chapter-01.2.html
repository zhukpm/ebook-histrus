Ancient Rus in the late X - early XII century

The basis of the economy of Ancient Rus was agriculture. People sowed rye, oats, wheat, buckwheat. Horses were highly valued as beasts of burden and military force (that is why Slavic farmers did not eat horsemeat). Foreign trade acquired particular importance. Rus sold furs, honey, flax, and bought weapons, expensive fabrics, as well as thoroughbred horses.

The bulk of the free population lived in villages. Farmers united in communities that regulated their activities and helped to survive in harsh environmental conditions. Boyars got individual villages as a reward for serving the prince. Over time, they turned into private property (patrimony). In Rus, there were also dependent people (kholops, etc.).

Ancient Rus reached its heyday under the son of Prince Vladimir - Y aroslav the Wise (1019-1054). During his reign, culture developed, the international authority of Rus grew. Many Christian monarchs of Europe dreamed of becoming related to the princes of Kiev. Thus, Anna, the daughter of Yaroslav the Wise, married the king of France.

Kiev became a major trade and cultural center. In the middle of the XI century, there was built the main temple of the Russian Orthodox Church, the Cathedral of St. Sophia , which was similar in beauty to the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople (the capital of Byzantium).

Under Yaroslav the Wise, there began the formulation of written laws - “Russkaya Pravda” (Russian Justice). The Laws regulated the behavior of people of that era, protected property. After the death of Yaroslav the Wise and the division of land between the brothers, there began intestine wars in the country, which led to the fragmentation (separation) of the Old Russian state.
