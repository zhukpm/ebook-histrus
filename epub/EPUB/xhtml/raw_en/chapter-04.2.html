Reign of Catherine II

Peter I became an example for Russian Empress Catherine II (1762-1796). She was of German origin, but she sincerely loved Russia. The Empress wanted to raise the level of the economic and cultural development of the country and to strengthen its position in the world. She was smart and well-educated. She believed in the power of education, and wanted everyone in her state to abide by the laws. It is noteworthy that she tried to avoid harsh methods of management.

Catherine II condemned serfdom, but she realized that its abolition would cause discontent among the nobility, which could dethrone her. Therefore, during the years of her reign serfdom did strengthen.

In 1767, Catherine wanted to adopt a new code of laws. To this end, she convened the Legislative Commission with the participation of deputies of all estates, but soon she dissolved it under the pretext of the outbreak of the war with Turkey.

In 1775, Catherine carried out the reform of local government. According to the territorial principle, Russia was divided into 50 provinces. Subsequently, the number of provinces increased, but the main provisions of the reform remained unchanged until 1917.

The policy of Catherine II in the socio-economic sphere was contradictory. On the one hand, serfdom was strengthened (for example, state peasants (i.e. personally free) could be given to landowners). However, at the same time, there were taken liberal measures. Representatives of all estates gained the right to engage in entrepreneurship. It contributed to the development of industrial production.

In 1785, the rights of the nobility were enshrined (Charter to the nobility): the right to land and peasants, exemption from compulsory service, etc. It is no accident that the era of the reign of Catherine II was called the “Golden Age” of the Russian nobility. Urban residents also gained limited rights (Charter to the towns).

During the reign of Catherine II, Russia significantly expanded its borders and gained access to the Black Sea. In addition to the new lands in the south (Novorossiya or New Russia), the country gained trade routes necessary for its economic development.

As a result of the wars against Turkey in the 1770-1780s, Russia annexed the territory of the Northern Black Sea Region, including the Crimean Peninsula (1783), where the base of the Black Sea Fleet - the city of Sevastopol - was established. It was Russian commander A.V. Suvorov who never lost a single battle and Admiral F.F. Ushakov that made a particular contribution to the defeat of the Turkish troops.

As a result, during the reign of Catherine II, the empire expanded significantly. In the west, it annexed the territories of Right-bank Ukraine, Belarus and parts of the Baltics. The Caucasus became part of Russia, which took Eastern Georgia under protection. There began the development of the Black Sea steppes. The international position of the country strengthened. Due to these successes, the contemporaries called their empress Catherine the Great.
