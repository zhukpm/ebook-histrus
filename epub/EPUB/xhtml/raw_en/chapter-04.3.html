Culture and life of Russia in the XVIII century

Culture and life of Russia in the XVIII century

It was the transformations of Peter I, as well as the expansion of relations with the countries of Western Europe that influenced the development of Russian culture in the XVIII century.

The state’s needs for specialists determined the need for establishing secular school . There appeared special educational institutions (mathematics, artillery, and other schools) for the army and navy. The first Russian textbooks were published. Printing was developing. In 1725 , Peter I signed the decree on the establishment of the Academy of Sciences .

Important changes occurred in everyday life: men (noblemen and townspeople) had to shave their beards and to be dressed like Europeans. From 1718, assemblies (balls) were held. There were developed special rules of behavior in society. Peter I allowed smoking. From 1700, the New Year began to be celebrated on January 1; at the same time, Russia switched to the chronology adopted in most European countries - from the birth of Christ .

The construction of St. Petersburg influenced the development of architecture. Initially, the Peter and Paul Fortress was built there (architect D. Trezini), which was supposed to protect the lands reconquered during the Great Northern War. The new capital of the Russian state acquired the appearance of a well-arranged European city.

In 1755 , according to the project of M.V. Lomonosov, there was established Moscow University, in which representatives of all estates could study.

In 1757 , the Academy of Arts was founded in St. Petersburg.

In the era of Catherine II, the role of the state in the educational system intensified. There were opened public schools. However, children of serfs still had no opportunity to get education.

From the middle of the XVIII century, the number of Russian scientists began to increase. Among them was outstanding scientist M.V. Lomonosov .

The main trend in the literature of the XVIII century was classicism with its desire for lofty moral ideals and heroic images. A prominent representative of classicism was A.P. Sumarokov , who wrote lyrical songs and tragedies. At the end of the century, a new literary and artistic movement was born - sentimentalism . It proclaimed the cult of feeling and nature. An outstanding representative of this trend was N.M. Karamzin .

In the middle of the XVIII century in architecture , the practicality and rationalism of the era of Peter I were replaced by the elegant style of palaces - the “High Baroque”, the most prominent representative of which in St. Petersburg was F.F. Rastrelli . He is the author of the famous Winter Palace (the Hermitage Museum).

It is the monument to Peter I that became the symbol of St. Petersburg (it is also called the “Bronze Horseman”, sculptor E. Falconet).

XVIII century, baroque was replaced by classicism , which was characterized by regular, clear lines and reference to ancient architecture. In Moscow, V.I. Bazhenov (Pashkov's house), M.F. Kazakov (the buildings of Moscow University and the Senate in the Kremlin) worked in this style.

In 1750, in Yaroslavl on the initiative of merchant F.G. Volkov , there emerged the first Russian drama professional theater .

Culturally, Russia definitively became one of the great European powers. This was facilitated by Empress Catherine herself, who was engaged in literary activities, collected masterpieces of painting and corresponded with French educators.
