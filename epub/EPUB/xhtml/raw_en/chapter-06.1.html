Economic and political development of Russia

Economic and political development of Russia

At the beginning of the XX century, the Russian Empire included the Baltics, Ukraine, Belarus, Poland, Finland, Transcaucasia, Kazakhstan and Central Asia. The population was 159 million people. Over 100 nations and nationalities lived in the territory of the empire. In Russia, there were 57% of non-Russians. A significant part of the population was Christians; most of them (about 75%) were Orthodox. In the western regions of Russia, there were mostly Catholics and Protestants. In different areas, there also lived a large group of Muslims. A part of the population professed Judaism and Buddhism.

In Russia, most inhabitants were peasants. Therefore, agriculture was developed. The Russian Empire ranked first in the world in grain production. It sold abroad 25% of the world exports of grain. In the country, there were also grown industrial crops: flax, sugar beets, tobacco. In Central Asia, a lot of cotton was grown. Livestock production was developing. A significant part of land belonged to landowners. In the central regions of Russia there dominated communal ownership of land.

Industry in Russia was developing rapidly. The country ranked4-5th in the world in terms of industrial production, but it lagged behind the advanced countries of the West. A large role in the development of the Russian industry was played by foreign companies. Banks were created. The main one was the State Bank.

In Russia, domestic and foreign trade was actively developing. Russia’s main trading partners were Germany and Great Britain. Abroad, Russia bought machinery and equipment. An important role in the development of the country was played by railway and water transport. Russia ranked second in the world (the USA ranked first) in terms of the length of railway lines.

At the same time, in Russia there remained absolute monarchy. It was the emperor that determined the policy of the government. After the death of Tsar Alexander III, his son Nicholas II (1894-1917) assumed the throne. Emperor Nicholas II was a conservative. He believed that reforms were dangerous for the existing order, and therefore, he did not want any changes. Nicholas II was the last Russian emperor. He was an educated man, he was very fond of his family, but he was indecisive and insufficiently prepared for governing the country.

In Russia, political parties were banned.

However, already at the end of the XIX century, in the western and southern regions of Russia there emerged the first national parties.

Later there appeared all-Russian revolutionary parties: the party of Russian Socialists-Revolutionaries (SRs),the Russian Social Democratic Labor Party (RSDLP), which actually split into two parties: the Bolsheviks and the Mensheviks . The leader of the Bolshevik Party was V.I. Lenin.

These parties wanted to improve the lives of ordinary people. They opposed the tsar, advocated a revolution in Russia and transfer of power to the people.

At the beginning of the XX century, in Russia the agrarian and labor issues were not resolved. Social inequality, political powerlessness, and the worsening of living conditions prompted people to oppose the existing order in the country. At that time, Nicholas II decided to start a war against Japan. He hoped the war would help strengthen his power.

The Russo-Japanese War began on January 25, 1904. The Japanese fleet attacked Russian ships off the coast of Japan (there was the Russian fortress of Port Arthur there). Japan defeated the Russian navy. In the course of the battles on land, the Russian troops could not stop the advance of the Japanese. In 1905, the war resulted in the victory of Japan. The defeat was mainly due to the weakness of the Russian military command, as well as poor condition of military equipment. During the war, many Russian sailors and officers perished. The war aggravated the political and economic crisis that set in motion the First Russian Revolution.
