Socio-economic and political development of Russia

Socio-economic and political development of Russia

After the Time of Troubles, already from the middle of the XVII century, thanks to the peaceful work of the Russian people, there began the growth of agricultural production. It was due to the development of fertile land in the center of Russia.

In the towns there was gradually being restored craft production. The first Russian manufactories appeared (by the end of the XVII century there were about 30 of them). The largest trade center was Moscow; fairs were of great significance. Foreign trade was traditional for Russia. It exported furs, grain, flax, leather, whereas it imported industrial products, tea, etc. Sea trade with Europe in the north was carried out through the only port – Arkhangelsk; it operated only a few months a year and could not satisfy the country's economic needs. Trade with eastern countries was carried out through Astrakhan. Moreover, Russia did not have its own fleet, and its foreign trade was in the hands of foreign merchants.

In the XVII century, there was established the social structure of Russian society, which was consolidated by the Sobornoye Ulozheniye(legal code) of 1649. These were service class people, who were in the public service, urban population and peasantry.

Peasants were the main part of the country's population; they were divided into free and dependent (serfs), who by the end of the XVII century made up more than 70% of rural residents. 

In the second half of the XVII century, the power of the tsar (autocracy) was strengthened. He no longer needed the support of the Zemsky Sobors. The Boyar Duma lost its significance as well. The tsar assembled the whole Duma less frequently; he discussed important issues with a narrow circle of people close to him.

In the middle of the XVII century, the reform of the Russian Orthodox Church began. The reform was caused by the desire of the state to strengthen the church. In addition, Russia tried to unite all the Orthodox churches under its leadership, and service was conducted in them according to the Greek rites.

The differences in the rites undermined the authority of the church. After Nikon was elected Patriarch (1652), the reform began to be implemented, especially in the sphere of the rites. For example, the tradition of crossing oneself with two fingers (two-fingered sign of the cross) was replaced by the tradition of crossing oneself with three fingers (three-fingered sign of the cross). The violent methods of implementing the reform led to the schism in the church. Some of the believers remained faithful to the old rites (therefore, they were called Old Believers or schismatics).

The XVII century was called the century of riots and popular unrest. There were many different town uprisings at that time. The most massive rioting of the townspeople was the Moscow uprising of 1648. It was caused by the tax reform, which raised prices for salt. The tsar reassured the people with great difficulty. The Sobornoye Ulozheniye of 1649 met a number of the citizens’ requirements. In the interests of the service class people, there was completed the enslavement of peasants.

The most massive popular rioting of the XVII century was the uprising led by Stepan Razin (1667-1671). There were involved Don Cossacks and peasants who had fled from the serfdom to the river Don. The rebels captured a number of Volga towns, but in October 1671 they were defeated by the tsar’s forces. The chieftain, Stepan Razin, was later captured and executed.
