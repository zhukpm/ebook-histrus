Reforms of Peter the Great. Emergence of the Russian Empire

By the end of the XVII century, in Russia the tsar’s power had strengthened, the population size had increased, and the borders of the state had expanded. However, the country was increasingly lagging behind Western Europe. In order to overcome the economic, military and cultural backwardness, it was necessary to carry out reforms and to establish trade relations with the leading countries of the world. However, the development of foreign trade was impossible without access to the Baltic Sea.

It was Peter I (1682-1725), one of the most famous historical figures of the country that played a huge role in solving the problems facing Russia. He always worked for the good of the fatherland (therefore he was called tsar-worker); he appreciated business qualities of people, regardless of their origin. It was serving the state that Peter made the purpose of his rule. However, to achieve his goals, he often used the most severe measures.

In 1700 , in order to gain access to the Baltic Sea, Russia began a bitter and lengthy war against Sweden - the Northern War (1700-1721).During that war, Peter I created a new army and navy and transformed the system of state management. The reforms in the field of culture and life created the conditions for the development of science, education, literature, etc.

Over the years of the reign of Peter I, over 200 manufactories were founded in the country. The Urals region became the center of the metallurgical industry, and in 1725 Russia ranked third in the world in the import of cast iron.

As industry was growing, there emerged a shortage of labor force at the factories. Therefore, in 1721 Peter allowed entrepreneurs, including non-noblemen, to buy serfs for the factories.

St. Petersburg , founded in 1703 on the Baltic Sea, became the center of foreign trade, and Peter I turned it into the new capital of the country. The state strengthened the position of the nobility. The decree of 1714 gave the right to inherit the estate only to one of the sons, which forced the rest to serve the state. The Law of 1722 (“Table of Ranks”) determined the procedure of performing civil service. Whether a nobleman could get a position depended on his education and personal abilities.

The tsar carried out the reforms with the help of peasants (92% of the population). Peasants built fortresses, factories, the new capital; they served in the army.

In 1724, a new tax was introduced. Every man, regardless of age (except for noblemen and priests that were privileged estates), was to pay taxes. As a result, tax burden increased two- or threefold. To prevent serfs from fleeing from their masters, the passport system was introduced.

During the reform of 1708-1710, the country was divided into 8 provinces headed by governors that were appointed by the tsar. There began the reform of the central government bodies. In 1711, instead of the Boyar Duma, the Senate was established. It performed the highest regulatory, judicial and law-advisory functions. In 1717-1722, there were formed colleges: Military, Foreign, and others. In 1721, the patriarchate was abolished. The Spiritual College (later the Synod ), led by a civil official that was appointed by the tsar, began to rule the church.

After defeating Sweden in 1721 and annexing the territory of the East Baltic region, Peter I got the title of emperor , and Russia became an empire .

The emperor was the bearer of the supreme legislative, executive and judicial powers and did not share it with anyone. From 1722, the emperor also had the right to appoint his successor.

As a result of the reforms that Peter I implemented, there were created powerful industrial production, a strong army and navy. It allowed Russia to gain access to the Baltic Sea, to close the gap with the advanced countries of Europe and become a great power.
