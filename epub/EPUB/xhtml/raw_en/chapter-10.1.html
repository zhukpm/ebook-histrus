Socio-economic development of the Russian Federation in the context of reforms

The Russian Federation is the largest state in the world. The territory of Russia is 17 million square kilometers. The country's population is more than 145 million people. Russia ranks ninth in terms of population in the world.

Moscow is the capital of Russia. It is the political and cultural center of modern Russia. There are located the parliament, the federal government, public institutions, governing bodies of political parties.

In terms of the number of inhabitants, Moscow is among 20 largest cities in the world along with London, Paris, New York, Tokyo. The population of Moscow is over 12 million people. It is the largest city in Russia. The main square of the Russian capital is Red Square.

Russia is a multinational country. Representatives of 160 nationalities live there. Most people profess Christianity (mainly Orthodoxy). The traditional religions are also Islam, Judaism, Buddhism.

At the same time, Russia is a secular state. Life in Russia is governed by civil rather than religious laws. The official language in the country is Russian. The republics within the Russian Federation also have the right to use their state languages, along with Russian. For example, in Chechnya it is the Chechen language, in Buryatia–the Buryat language, in Tatarstan–the Tatar language.

On June 12, 1990 , there was adopted the Declaration of State Sovereignty of the RSFSR. This day became a state holiday – Russia Day. In 1991, B.N. Yeltsin became the first President of Russia (1991-1999). Economic reforms began in the country. The main goal of the reforms was to establish a market economy in Russia.

The results of the reforms were very different. In the country, there were introduced free prices ( price liberalization) . There began privatization, i.e. the transfer of state property to private property. The reforms created new opportunities for people. Some of them got good jobs at state and private enterprises. There appeared private business in the banking and financial sectors; a new class appeared - the middle class which supports market reforms. It helped to develop the economy.

There appeareda lot of different goods and food. However, the cost of goods became higher, wages decreased. As a result of the economic reforms, the population was divided into the rich and the poor; many people's lives became worse.

By the end of the 1990s, the economic indicators in Russia had worsened. The production of manufactured goods and food decreased. There began the demographic crisis: over the decade of the reforms the population of Russia also decreased.

Under the influence of the economic problems in the country, people began to protest against price increase, closing of factories and plants. The problems in implementing the reforms led to the weakening of the state.

In August 1999, V.V. Putin became the new Prime Minister. By that time, the economy of Russia had become weaker than in 1990, i.e. before the collapse of the USSR.
